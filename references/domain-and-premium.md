# Domain Lens & Premium Decomposition

Used in Phase 2. A two-stage chain, in this order — the second stage is
impossible without the first:

1. **Domain lens** — acquire the category's real quality markers, because they're
   different for every category and invisible to a layperson.
2. **Premium decomposition** — once you can see the substance, work out how much
   of the price is substance and how much is brand 溢价 (markup).

## Stage 1 — Become the category's expert

This is the soul of the whole skill: knowledge-based decisions mean reasoning
from genuine domain expertise, not surveying a category from the outside. For the
length of the decision, *become* the relevant expert — the textile and
garment-construction specialist, the horticulturist, the display engineer who
could have worked on Samsung's panels. Adopt the practitioner's perspective and
reason from inside the field.

There is no universal "quality." Each category has its own body of expert
knowledge — the markers practitioners look at — that marketing actively hides
behind a proxy (the brand name, the price, the packaging, one hero spec). The
standard to reach is "a professional in this field would recognise my reasoning
as their own," not "a well-read shopper." Research live to that depth; don't rely
on a stored list. Illustrative lenses (derive the real one for the actual
category):

- **Clothing:** fibre type and grade, fabric weight (GSM), weave/knit, the cut
  and pattern (版型), construction and sewing (缝纫 — seam type, stitch density,
  finishing, lining, hardware), and fit. A cheap logo tee and an expensive one
  can be the same blank; the difference (if any) is here.
- **Skincare:** active ingredients and their *concentration*, ingredient order
  (INCI), formulation and delivery, pH, evidence for the actives, irritants and
  filler, and packaging that protects the actives (air/light stability). The
  chemistry is the product; the jar is not.
- **Furniture/wood:** solid wood vs veneer vs particleboard, joinery (dovetail vs
  staples), species, finish.
- **Knives/tools:** steel type and hardness, grind and edge geometry,
  construction (e.g. full tang).
- **Food/coffee/tea:** origin, processing, freshness, grade.

(Electronics' lens — chipset, panel, standards — is largely handled by
market-scan.md.)

For non-objects the expertise reinterprets: a **service's** "quality markers" are
a provider's track record, credentials, and scope clarity (the expert is someone
who knows how the trade vets its own); an **experience's** are the things that
actually shape it versus the marketed extras; a **financial product's** are the
fee/term structures that decide real cost. Become whichever expert the type calls
for (see `purchase-types-and-scenarios.md`).

**Stakes gate.** Acquire only as much lens as the decision warrants. Enough to
see the markers that matter for *this* use and budget — not a connoisseur's
education for a throwaway. Over-learning is the paralysis trap again.

## Stage 2 — Decompose the price: substance vs premium

A price is a stack: materials + construction/labour + genuine R&D/function +
brand premium + marketing/ad spend + retail margin. The person wants to pay for
the substance and the function, not for the logo and the ad budget. The question
for any price: **which part is the stuff, and which part is the name?**

(For non-objects the same question reframes: for a service, substance vs the firm's
prestige and overhead — is the name-brand firm's work actually better, or just
dearer than a solo practitioner doing the same job? For a subscription or
financial product, the "premium" is the fees and terms over the holding period
versus what a leaner option delivers. Decompose accordingly.)

You can't get exact cost-of-goods, but you can triangulate:

1. **Assess the substance** using the Stage-1 lens — is the material/
   construction/formulation actually high, mid, or low?
2. **Find the unbranded equivalent price** — the same factory output without the
   name (white-label/OEM/generic; see value-curve.md). This approximates
   substance + construction cost.
3. **The gap** between the branded price and that equivalent — adjusted for any
   *real* quality difference the lens revealed — is the brand premium.
4. **Ask what the premium buys.** This is the judgment, and it is symmetric:

   - **Premium is earned when** it buys real QC consistency (the cheap version is
     a quality lottery), a genuine design/fit/formulation advantage the lens
     confirms, durable warranty and after-sales, resale value that recovers part
     of the markup, or signalling the person actually needs (an uncomfortable but
     real function in some professional/social contexts).
   - **Premium is waste when** the substance is identical to the unbranded version
     (pure white-label), when you're paying for ad spend and a logo, or when the
     "premium" features are hype (see hype-filter.md).

   Do **not** assume all premium is waste. Pure-name premium and earned premium
   look identical on the price tag — only the domain lens tells them apart. That
   is the whole reason Stage 1 comes first.

## Output

Tell the person, in a line, where their money is going: "roughly, you're paying
for [substance X] which is real, plus a brand premium of about [Y] that buys
[something / nothing here]." That sentence is often the most useful thing the
whole skill produces.
