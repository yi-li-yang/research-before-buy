# Value Curve, Spec Translation, Brand Decoding

Use during Phase 2, once the axis is known and a *vertical* (tier) decision is
live. If the decision is horizontal, settle that first (see axis-selector.md);
this method does not apply across kinds.

## Finding the knee

Every product category has a price-performance curve with a knee — the point
past which price climbs faster than value. The skill of the tier decision is
locating that knee **for this person's use**, not the market's average use.

- Overshoot the knee → wasted money.
- Undershoot → regret.

To find it live:
1. Search the category's actual current tiers (entry / mid / premium / luxury)
   with representative current models and prices — never quote from memory.
2. Identify what each tier-jump *buys*. Sort those gains into "matters for this
   use" vs "marketing for a different user."
3. The knee sits just above the last jump that still matters to this person.

## Spec → experience translation

The person came here because they can't map spec-sheet jargon to lived
experience. That translation is the deliverable, not the spec list. For each
spec that matters to their use, say what it *feels like* in practice and at what
threshold the difference stops being noticeable. Drop specs that don't bear on
their use entirely, even impressive-sounding ones — naming them only feeds
overwhelm.

## Brand decoding

A brand encodes several things at once, and they pull in different directions:
reliability, repairability, resale value, ecosystem lock-in, warranty *quality*
(not just length) — and sometimes pure marketing premium.

**White-label / OEM reality.** A large fraction of consumer goods, especially
from China, are the same factory (ODM/OEM) output sold under many stickers.
Where this is the case, the "which brand" question partly dissolves: the cheaper
sticker on the identical product is the rational buy, and the premium sticker is
paying for the label. Check for this before treating brands as meaningfully
different — it collapses a lot of agonizing.

## What not to do

- Don't hand back a generic ranking. Hand back tiers tied to *this* use.
- Don't quote prices or model names from memory; they decay monthly. Search.
- Don't equate price with quality, especially across white-labeled goods.
