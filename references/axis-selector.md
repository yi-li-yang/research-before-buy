# Axis Selector

The core asset of this skill. Before researching anything, identify which
dimension the decision actually turns on. This file is the repertoire of axes to
select among. They are patterns, not an exhaustive list — add to it as real use
reveals new ones.

A decision can live on more than one axis at once. Read for the *dominant* one
first; it usually collapses the others.

## How to use this file

1. From what the person said, hypothesize the axis.
2. Confirm with at most one sharp question if genuinely uncertain.
3. Route to the matching pattern below. Each tells you what to do *instead* of a
   generic value comparison.

---

## Axis 1 — Horizontal vs Vertical (resolve horizontal first)

**Vertical:** tiers of the same kind of thing (cheap vs premium monitor). The
value-curve method applies — find the knee.

**Horizontal:** different *kinds* of thing wearing one category name (cast iron
vs nonstick vs ceramic vs stainless pans; mechanical vs membrane keyboards).
These are different tools optimized for different jobs. There is no "best" — the
question secretly assumes one item should do everything, which guarantees either
paralysis or a bad compromise.

For horizontal decisions:
- Do **not** rank the kinds or build a four-way pros/cons matrix (that's the
  paralysis the person walked in with, reorganized).
- De-average the debate: work out what each camp is actually doing with the
  thing, then locate the person among them.
- The honest answer may be **more than one item**, each owning a use. The
  all-in-one hybrid that does everything adequately and nothing well is the
  horizontal version of the middle-trap (see Axis 2).
- Only after a kind is chosen does the vertical decision (which tier within it)
  begin.

## Axis 2 — Budget as an output, not an input

Never ask "what's your budget." A person can hold two coherent strategies at
once (spend the minimum to get it done, or invest heavily for the long term),
and asking for a number forces a premature collapse. Instead derive the spend
from two questions:

1. **Means or engagement?** Do they want only the *outcome* (clean clothes, a
   drilled hole — the object is invisible to them), or do they *interact with
   the object itself* as part of the experience?
2. **Proven or testing?** Do they already know they'll keep using this, or are
   they still finding out whether they even want it?

The four cells, and the spend each implies:

| | Testing | Proven |
|---|---|---|
| **Means** | minimum / probe / borrow | **workhorse — the one case the middle tier is right**: optimize durability-per-dollar + total cost of ownership |
| **Engagement** | cheap probe, upgrade after commitment | **investment** — buy at or above the knee; quality is felt daily and amortizes |

**The barbell.** For the *engagement* row, "either cheap-throwaway or real-
quality, never the middle" is correct: the middle is expensive enough to hurt,
not good enough to last or to love. Recognize and back this instinct — it is
sound, not indecision. But it **flips on the means row**, where the middle is
exactly right. The rule: *avoid the middle when you engage with the object; aim
for it when you only want the outcome.*

Record a hard budget cap only if a real wall exists ("I physically cannot exceed
X"). Target is derived; cap is elicited.

## Axis 3 — Stakes below the noise floor

When the person is genuinely price-insensitive at this scale (e.g. 10 vs 100
yuan and they don't care) and risk is low, the value-curve machinery is the
wrong tool — optimizing price-to-value when price doesn't bind solves a problem
they don't have. Switch the axis from **price-to-value** to **risk-and-friction**:

1. **Interrogate "both work."** It usually means "functions out of the box,"
   not "serves me well over the lifetime I need." The 10× often shows up later
   in durability or in *how it fails* (a cheap charger that fails unsafely).
2. **Ask what the premium actually buys** — one of four, pointing opposite ways:
   - *Pure brand tax / white-label:* identical product → buy cheap; the premium
     gives nothing.
   - *Real durability:* the true cost is the re-buy loop (dying at a bad moment,
     re-ordering, waiting) → if price-insensitive but hassle-sensitive, buy
     durable.
   - *Safety / tail risk* (electrical, load-bearing, touches the body): premium
     is cheap insurance → buy up, don't deliberate.
   - *Daily-use finish:* nicer to use every day → cheap quality-of-life, buy
     nicer.
3. **The asymmetry has flipped.** Normally you hunt the knee to avoid
   overpaying. When overpaying doesn't hurt, the only thing that *can* hurt is
   going cheap and hitting failure or risk → default tilts toward the reliable
   option. "Saving" money the person has already said is meaningless is the
   actual irrationality here.
4. **The one exception:** if the *need itself* is still fuzzy, buy the cheap one
   deliberately as a **probe** to learn what's actually wanted, then upgrade.

Compressed tree: need fuzzy → cheap probe; premium buys safety/durability → buy
up; premium is pure brand tax → buy cheap; trivial + low-risk → coin-flip and
stop.

## Axis 4 — Divergence as signal

When opinions split violently on a category, the divergence is usually *not*
noise or half the internet being wrong — both loud camps are right for their own
use and talking past each other. Treat strong divergence as evidence the category
serves several non-overlapping needs. Don't try to settle the argument or side
with the loudest camp; de-average it (often this is Axis 1 in disguise) and find
which side is this person's kitchen.

## Axis 5 — Objective vs subjective (the taste ceiling)

Some decisions have a knowable better answer; some are pure preference or values,
where there is no "right" — only what this person likes or believes. Most real
purchases are a mix, and the move is to find the line between the two within the
decision.

- The **objective part** — what's well made vs badly made, what the terms really
  cost, what fails — is the expert's job to settle.
- The **subjective part** — which colour, which cut, which flavour, which ethical
  trade-off — is the person's, and expertise cannot decide it without becoming
  presumptuous.

So: rule out the badly made, frame the genuinely-good options and their
trade-offs, then **hand the subjective choice back explicitly.** Manufacturing a
single "right answer" where there's only preference is over-rationalising — a
failure mode, not rigour. Strongest for experiences, aesthetics, hobbies, and
values-driven buys.

## Adding axes

When real use surfaces a decision that none of the above frames cleanly, that's
a new axis — write it here in the same shape (name → when it applies → what to do
*instead of* a generic comparison). The skill's spine (diagnose the axis, then
act) never changes; the repertoire grows.
