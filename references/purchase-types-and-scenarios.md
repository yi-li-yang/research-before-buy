# Purchase Types & Corner-Case Scenarios

The skill's lenses were first written for physical objects, but "buying" spans
far more. **Recognise the type first** — it decides which lenses apply and how
they reinterpret. Then check the cross-cutting scenarios, which can overlay any
type. This file is the coverage backbone; consult it early (Phase 1).

## Part A — Purchase types

For each type, the same four questions get different answers: what "quality"
means, what the "premium" is, what the "channel" (how you acquire it) is, and
what the dominant risk is.

### Object (physical good) — the default
- Quality = materials + construction. Premium = brand vs white-label. Channel =
  where/country (see `sourcing-uk-china.md`). Risk = defects, durability,
  counterfeit. Timing = product cycle. All existing lenses apply directly.

### Service (contractor, tradesperson, professional, repair, cleaning, salon)
- You are buying a *provider's future performance*, not a SKU — and variability
  between providers of the "same" service is the central risk, far larger than
  between units of a product.
- Quality = track record, references, portfolio, credentials, and the clarity of
  scope/contract. Premium = a name-brand firm vs a solo practitioner — often you
  pay for overhead and reputation while the actual work is done by the same kind
  of person, sometimes better at the smaller shop. Channel = *which provider, found
  how* — a vetted referral beats a marketplace beats an ad.
- No returns: you commit before you see the output, so **recourse** (contract,
  deposit terms, dispute path) matters more than for goods. The insider lens is
  gold here — how does the trade vet its own?

### Experience (travel, dining, events, a course taken for its own sake)
- Largely subjective, one-shot, non-returnable, time-bound, valued as memory.
  Can't be A/B tested. The value curve is about *diminishing experiential return*
  — how much better the premium version honestly is.
- Timing and dynamic pricing dominate (flights, hotels). Regret is asymmetric
  (missing out vs overpaying). The taste/subjectivity boundary (below, and in
  `axis-selector.md`) is strongest here — inform, don't settle.

### Subscription / recurring (SaaS, streaming, gym, membership, recurring insurance)
- The decision is *ongoing*, not one-shot. **Total cost over the realistic
  holding period** dominates the sticker price. Core risks: lock-in, switching
  cost, auto-renewal and cancellation friction.
- Always check the exit before the entrance (how do you cancel, what does it
  cost). The goal-reframe is central: do you need it at all, is it bundled in
  something you already pay for, is annual cheaper than monthly. Premium tier =
  the features you'll actually use, not the ones listed.

### Financial product (credit card, loan, mortgage, insurance, investment account)
- Regulated. "Premium" = fees, interest, and terms; quality = coverage/terms
  fit. Risk = real financial harm (debt, fees, credit-score impact, mis-fit).
  Comparison is APR / fees / coverage / terms, not "quality."
- **Boundary: inform, do not give regulated personalised advice.** Lay out how the
  products differ and what to check; for anything significant (mortgage, large
  loan, investment, insurance with health implications) recommend a qualified
  adviser. Watch teaser/intro rates (the tech-currency analogue) and cost over the
  realistic holding period, not the headline.

### Digital good / licence (software, games, ebooks, online courses)
- No physical sourcing. Licensing model is the crux: perpetual vs subscription,
  region-lock, platform lock-in, account/DRM risk. Channel = official store vs key
  reseller (legitimacy/scam risk). Free or open-source alternatives often exist —
  the goal-reframe matters.

## Part B — Cross-cutting scenarios (can overlay any type)

### Gift / buying for someone else
- The need is *their* use, known imperfectly — the "elicit from how you'll use
  it" rule breaks. Elicit what's known about them; weight broad appeal, and
  return-ability/gift-receipt, over optimisation. Don't over-spec a stranger's
  taste. Often the safest gift is the one they can exchange, or an experience they
  choose.

### Repair vs replace · rent vs buy · buy at all (belongs in the goal-reframe)
- Always put these on the table. Rough rules: repair if it costs well under
  replacement and the rest of the item has life left; rent/borrow if use is rare
  or temporary; don't buy if the job is genuinely one-off. "Do nothing" is a real
  answer.

### Used / second-hand
- Condition variance dominates spec. The protective move is inspection or
  verification before committing. Scam vectors: too-cheap, off-platform payment,
  refusal of inspection. There's often a depreciation sweet spot — let the first
  owner eat the steepest drop.

### Negotiable or dynamic pricing (cars, property, markets, B2B; flights, hotels)
- The price is a *move*, not a fact. Don't treat a quote as the value. For
  negotiable: leverage, walk-away point, timing. For dynamic: booking windows and
  volatility. Surface the realistic transacted price, not the asking price.

### Urgent / emergency
- No time to optimise. Compress to "safe, fast, good-enough, reversible if
  possible." Avoid the panic-priced buy and the predatory urgency upsell — you can
  optimise the *permanent* replacement later, once the fire is out.

### Ethics / values-driven
- When the dominant axis is sustainability, labour, origin, or animal welfare,
  treat values as a first-class constraint, research the *actual* practices (watch
  greenwashing — pair with `hype-filter.md`), and accept the person may rationally
  pay more for alignment. Don't override their values with pure price/value logic.

### Taste-dominated (art, decor, a garment's cut, a hobby's "feel")
- Expertise can rule out the badly made and frame the real options, but it
  **cannot pick for them**. Present the informed shortlist and explicitly hand the
  final subjective choice back. Over-rationalising taste is a failure mode, not
  thoroughness.
